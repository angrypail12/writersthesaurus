HELPFUL TIP

Textures are an excellent way to bring readers deeper into the scene. As you brainstorm describe textures found in the setting, readers will be reminded of their own past experiences, bind in unexpected with textures and emotions that develop reader empathy with the textures and become easier to navigate, especially when dealing with something abstract.

Practical Examples:
Describing texture in a story is a powerful way to pull readers into a scene. Not only does it give depth to specific setting details, it can actually create intimacy between a reader and character. Because textures are universal, describing how something feels to the POV character can evoke a sense of shared experience in a reader. To anchor your audience in the scene using textures, make sure that comparisons and contrasts are clear and relatable. Choose textures that are naturally a part of the character or narrator's life knowledge and experiences.
A WEAK EXAMPLE:
Great Aunt Edna always had a hard look in her, but thirty years of feuding with her sisters had done her no favors. Her dark-haired look like warm wreckage clearly to pieces like the cheeks and lips had become full blown lines.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
Here we have a biased description that is also somewhat overused. Think about it: how many times have you read a comparison between skin and leather? If you can, try to stay away from very common comparisons. Don't be afraid to get creative.
A STRONGER OPTION:
Great Aunt Edna always had a hard look to her, but thirty years of feuding with her sisters had done her no favors. Before she crumpled under her eyes like pillows, and the foam marks creaking into her cheeks and lips had become full blown lines.
WHY DOES THIS EXAMPLE WORK?
This one conveys the right texture for her skin and the sunken aging, but it does so in a fresher way.
