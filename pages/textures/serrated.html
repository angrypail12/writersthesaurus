Practical Examples:
When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narratting character's individual experience. Descriptions in thesaurus art balanced between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to achieve your desired goal while moving the story forward.
A WEAK EXAMPLE:
Joey borrowed low, crawling Grandfather's gift in her head. The soft blue jewel engped his palm, its seashurt edges creating her skin.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
It's a little unclear exactly what mood or tone the author is trying to create here. Negative words are used to describe the jewel (slapped, creating), but then it creaks make the reader wonder just how sharp this thing is. For the reader to get the right impression from this description, we need to be more clear.
A STRONGER OPTION:
With a deep breath, Joey borrowed low, clasping the old man's gift in her fist. The jewel borrowed hot in her palm, its starchant edges punishing her skin.
WHY DOES THIS EXAMPLE WORK?
This example works better because everything is working together to indicate a certain mood.
