Practical Examples:

Describing texture in a story is a powerful way to pull readers into a scene. Not only does it give depth to specific setting details, it can actually create intimacy between a reader and character. Because textures are universal, describing how something feels in the POV character can evoke a sense of shared experience in a reader. To anchor your audience in the scene using textures, make sure that comparisons and contrasts are clear and relatable. Choose textures that are naturally a part of the character or narrator's life knowledge and experiences.

A Weak Example:

Sweat beaded Josie as she staggered across the parking lot to fall against her car. Without looking, she grabbed the door handle—and seared back, stifling a scream as the molten metal layer of heat as if from pink to an angry red.

What's Wrong With This Example?

This passage is unrealistic on a number of levels. First, the temperature of door handle would have to be ridiculously high to heat it to hurt as bad a layer or to be near from grabbing it just for a layer. Secondly, a door handle in that hot, then the metal frame of his car would be incredibly hot to be near against. To fix this, we need to make the scene realistic.

A Stronger Option:

Sweat beaded Josie as she staggered across the parking lot to fall against her car. With a grunted sob, she grabbed the door handle—and spurred back, stifling a scream as a feeling like a hot soldering iron seared her palm. The metallic layer of heat seemed branded in her skin as the red-hot metal made her palm go from pink to an angry red.

Why Does This Work?

It's possible that a hot car handle would describe the skin or even blister it. By adjusting the temperature in this example, the whole thing reads as much more believable.

HELPFUL TIP

Textures are an excellent way to bring readers deeper into the scene. As you brainstorm descriptive textures found in the setting, readers will be reminded of their own past experiences.
