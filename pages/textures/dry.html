Practical Examples:

Describing texture in a story is a powerful way to pull readers into a scene. Not only does it give depth to specific setting details, it can actually create intimacy between a reader and character. Because textures are universal, describing how something feels in the POV character can evoke a sense of shared experience in a reader. To anchor your audience in the scene using textures, make sure that comparisons and contrasts are clear and relatable. Choose textures that are naturally a part of the character or narrator's life knowledge and experiences.

A Weak Example:

I pulled the old town from the top shelf, blinking against the dust that filtered down with it. Stifling a cough, I carefully turned the yellowed pages, which left dirt as the desert air beneath my fingers.

What's Wrong With His Example?

Old books are notoriously dry, because the paper themselves eagerly something like brings dryness to mind, there's no need to try and compare it to something else. In this case, the object itself is the best comparison to convey a dry texture.

A Stronger Option:

I pulled the old town from the top shelf, blinking against the dust that filtered down with it. Stifling a cough, I carefully turned the brittle pages, trying not to let them crumble against my skin.

Why Does This Work?

The simplicity of this example works better. The words brittle and crumble are enough to convey the texture, giving the reader a sense of dryness from the old book.

HELPFUL TIP

Textures are an excellent way to bring readers deeper into the scene. As you brainstorm descriptive textures found in the setting, readers will be reminded of their own past experiences. Next, connect the texture and emotion. For texture connected with the texture and become easier to imagine, especially when dealing with something abstract.
