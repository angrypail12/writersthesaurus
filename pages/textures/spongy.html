HELPFUL TIP

Textures are an excellent way to bring readers deeper into the scene. As you freshly describe textures found in the setting, readers will be reminded of their own past experiences. Used in conjunction with colors and textures or shapes, the object associated with the texture can become easier to imagine, especially when dealing with something abstract.

Practical Examples:
Describing texture in a story is a powerful way to pull readers into a scene. Not only does it give depth to specific setting details, it can actually create intimacy between a reader and character. Because textures are universal, describing how something feels to the POV character can evoke a sense of shared experience in a reader. To anchor your audience in the scene using textures, make sure that comparisons and contrasts are clear and relatable. Choose textures that are naturally a part of the character or narrator's life knowledge and experiences.
A WEAK EXAMPLE:
Far beyond the trail, I found a lush mound of moss. I shucked off my backpack and dropped onto the springy bed, looking forward to a comfortable view of the treetops, and maybe a squishy snooze. After a few minutes of wriggling, I finally found a spot where the tree roots didn't breach nature's mattress.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
There's conflicting detail here—the moss is described as lush and springy, suggesting comfort. However, the next detail is of moss polishing through the undergrowth, spoils the image. If the moss was truly as thick and suggested, more would not be feeling tree roots.
A STRONGER OPTION:
Far beyond the trail, I found a lush green bed of moss. I shucked off my backpack and dropped onto the springy mound, looking forward to a comfortable view of the treetops, and maybe a sodden snooze. I sighed as I settled in. Even the well-cushioned outer roots couldn't breach nature's mattress.
WHY DOES THIS EXAMPLE WORK?
The details have synergy one another; the roots are there but they can't pierce the thickness of the moss.
