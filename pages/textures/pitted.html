Practical Examples:

Describing texture in a story is a powerful way to pull readers into a scene. Not only does it give depth to specific setting details, it can actually create intimacy between a reader and character. Because textures are universal, describing how something feels in the POV character can evoke a sense of shared experience in a reader. To anchor your audience in the scene using textures, make sure that comparisons and contrasts are clear and relatable. Choose textures that are naturally a part of the character or narrator's life knowledge and experiences.

A Weak Example:

Janice smiled at her date, taking note of his expensive suit and gold watch. With a plated rock map of a face that silenced her long history with acne, he wasn't the best looking guy. But he obviously had cash. His slumped shoulders and hopeful gave said he'd have no trouble wedding all over him.

What's Wrong With This Example?

This isn't bad, but it's a bit weak because the condition of his face is explained rather than compared or contrasted to something with a similar texture.

A Stronger Option:

Janice smiled at her date, taking note of his expensive suit and gold watch. Not the best looking guy, with the slumping bear and hopeful gave said he'd have no trouble wedding all over him.

Why Does This Example Work?

This comparison has nice symmetry. Not only does the handsome comparison create a good image of what his skin looks like, it provides a nice image into the psychological description of one character while giving insight into the personality of the other.

HELPFUL TIP

Textures are an excellent way to bring readers deeper into the scene. As you brainstorm descriptive textures found in the setting, readers will be reminded of their own past experiences. Next, connect the texture and emotion. For texture connected with the texture and become easier to imagine, especially when dealing with something abstract.
