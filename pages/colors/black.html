Practical Examples:

Colors are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right color comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a color comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the color choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:
Once Wendy loaded up at the buffet, she sashayed to our table, smiling like she actually thought she belonged. She'd chosen a short ebony dress as her outfit this evening, which made her bloated white legs as appealing as soiled hospital linen.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
This passage places more emphasis on how the black skirt makes her legs look, when it should be about describing the skirt itself. If the black object is important, don't let other details overrun the description.
A STRONGER OPTION:
Once Wendy loaded up at the buffet, she sashayed to our table, smiling like she actually thought she belonged. As she stood there balancing a heaped plate, I had to stop myself from laughing at her ridiculous ebony dress. Did she really think those sequins were doing her rolls of fat any favors? She looked like a sack of tires that someone sprayed in glitter.
WHY IS THIS EXAMPLE BETTER?
This description better describes Wendy's attire while also conveying the nastiness of the POV character who is making the observation.

Helpful Tip:
Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as motifs to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
