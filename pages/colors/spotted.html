Practical Examples:

Colors and patterns are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right pattern comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:

Once the snow melted, Dan finally got his first look at the backyard of his new home... and it wasn't pretty. The grass was more spotted than an overripe banana. Mary had been right; they should never have bought a place owned by dog breeders.

What's wrong with this example?

It's a little weak: a banana is small, a yard is big. Also, by the description, readers won't know if the spots are holes dug by dogs or bald spots caused by urine. We need something that sends a good, concrete image, leaving no doubt as to the yard's poor condition.

A STRONGER OPTION:

Once the snow melted, Dan finally got his first look at the backyard of his new home. It wasn't pretty. Pet marks scabbed the grass like some splotchy disease and there was so much poop he couldn't count it all. Mary had been right; they should have never bought a place owned by dog breeders.

Why is this example better?

This example allows scene freedom because we aren't trying to match the size of the yard with something comparable. It also sets the emotional mood by using a comparison that's distasteful.

Symbolism associated with this pattern:

illness, abundance

Helpful Tip

Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as mood to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
