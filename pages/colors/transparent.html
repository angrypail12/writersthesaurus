Synonyms for this color:

clear, see-through, translucent, sheer

Practical Examples:

Colors are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right color comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a color comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the color choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:

Jenny polished the glass door, wiping away fingerprints and dirt smudges until the see-through surface was actually clear. She did such a stellar job, in fact, that four people ran into it before management decided to prep it open.

What's wrong with this example?

Transparent surfaces are difficult to describe as a color because they're mostly an absence of color. Trying to do so can make the details clunky and distracting. In this case, describing the object's function or effect will better serve the scene.

A STRONGER OPTION:

Jenny polished the glass door, wiping away fingerprints and dirt smudges. She did such a stellar job, in fact, that four people ran into it before management decided to prep it open.

Why is this example better?

This shows us the door's transparent state without getting too complicated or wordy.

Symbolism associated with this color:

honesty, clarity, simplicity

Helpful Tip

Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as mood to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
