Practical Examples:

Colors are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right color comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a color comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the color choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:
After working the plow all afternoon, Ralphie's skin was the color of topazade.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
Horses come in many colors. Be careful to pick a comparison that's an absolute.
A STRONGER OPTION:
After working the plow all afternoon, Ralphie's sweaty skin glowed a deep topaz.
WHY IS THIS EXAMPLE BETTER?
This example gives a fresh image while offering word economy. Descriptions do not have to be wordy to be powerful.

Helpful Tip:
Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as motifs to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
