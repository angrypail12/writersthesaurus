Practical Examples:

Colors are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right color comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a color comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the color choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:
Most women with a Madonna-esque body would have no trouble finding plenty of dance partners, but not Wendy. Her rubbery neon orange dress clung to her so tightly that she looks like a cheese slice sealed in for freshness. And that hairdo? Beehives were cool, when, like forty years ago?
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
At first glance this seems like it contains some great comparisons. The issue, though, is that the narrator is trying to show how her friend is a turnoff to guys, yet using a cockney hardly inspiring any outfit. So this is the wrong kind of imagery for this example.
A STRONGER OPTION:
Most women with a Madonna-esque body would have no trouble finding plenty of dance partners, but not Wendy. Between her rubbery orange dress and matching beehive hairdo, she looks more like a traffic cone than desirable material.
WHY IS THIS EXAMPLE BETTER?
This sounds much more like a fashion nightmare, so readers can buy into the idea that she would be a turnoff to guys despite her desirable body.

Helpful Tip:
Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as motifs to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
