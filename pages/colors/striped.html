Practical Examples:

Colors and patterns are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right pattern comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:

Megan's tight white dress with its strange pattern of uneven black slashes made her look like a zebra.

What's wrong with this example?

This description choice is a bit obvious. A black and white dress, a zebra... it feels too generic. Think outside the box.

A STRONGER OPTION:

Megan's narrow white dress with its pattern of uneven black slashes made her look like the bar code on a bottle of discount shampoo.

Why is this example better?

Our goal is to show her poor taste in an outfit and so the unusual comparison here works.

Symbolism associated with this pattern:

unity, complexity

Helpful Tip

Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as mood to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
