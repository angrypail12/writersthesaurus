Practical Examples:

Colors are powerful descriptors, not filters. The right color comparison with a man-made or natural object will help paint an immediate picture, helping readers see the setting in a way that keeps the pace moving. When using something man-made or natural as a color comparison, choose one that is not only accurate and known to readers but also fits the mood. To feel authentic, the color choice should match the viewpoint character or narrator's history, education, and range of experiences.

A WEAK EXAMPLE:
When Jasper said his cousin was coming to visit, I figured he'd be like the rest of the farm boys: sturdy and dumb as an ox. Boy, was I wrong. Forest-dark hair and blue eyes made me lose my grip so much that I forgot to say goodbye to my boyfriend first. You could be the green of our yard got greener before we mowed it.
WHAT'S WRONG WITH THIS EXAMPLE?
The style of writing here suggests the narrator is trying to show how hip friend is to a turnoff in guys, yet using a cousin barely skips a beat over letting her boyfriend first. You could be as green as our yard got greener before we mowed is awkward.
A STRONGER OPTION:
When Jasper said his cousin was coming to visit, I figured he'd be like the rest of the farm boys: sturdy and dumb as an ox. Boy, was I wrong. Forest-dark hair and blue eyes made me lose my grip so much that Josh would have been green being out of the loop if he'd seen me doing.
WHY IS THIS EXAMPLE BETTER?
This example works because the comparison is a very famous part, helping them form an immediate picture.

Helpful Tip:
Color is important to the human experience, meaning readers are hardwired to notice it. When description is necessary, think about how your color and pattern choices may be used as motifs to help symbolize an idea you want to subtly reinforce in the setting description.
