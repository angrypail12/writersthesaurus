THUNDERSTORM ☆

HELPFUL TIP:

Don't be afraid to pair weather conditions with your setting choice to contrast or reinforce a character's emotions. Replacements might be mirrored in the relentless drizzle of rain and the resulting heavy drag of one's clothes; alternatively, the feeling can stand out in stark contrast to sunlight dancing over fresh snow. Can weather also provide symbolism, thereby enriching the reader's experience? Think about your goal for the scene and the mood you are trying to create, and then layer your description with meaningful choices.

SIGHTS:
  • Heavy gray or black clouds that block the light
  • A strong wind that shakes and whips trees back and forth
  • Rain pelting the ground in such volumes that it floods
  • Dirty runoff streams racing down gutters and slopes
  • Puddles that appear to jump from high-velocity rain drops
  • Pools of dirty rainwater forming in low spots
  • Rainfall so pronounced it creates cloud formations
  • Raindrops and sewer grates
  • Lightning flashes that stab downward or light up the clouds
  • A false darkness that makes the setting feel oppressive and washed-out
  • Clouds that boil across the sky in a bruise-colored wall
  • Flower petals littering the grass
  • Shadows and poor visibility
  • People flinching as the boom of thunder hits
  • Dogs howling or hiding from the noise
  • A sudden darkness as power goes out inside a home
SMELLS:
  • Air heavy with moisture
  • Wet earth and grass
  • A fresh clean smell following the storm
  • Ozone
TASTES:
Rainwater
TEXTURES AND SENSATIONS:
  • Air that feels heavy
  • Wind that whips at one's hair and clothes
  • Rain that drenches one's clothing and plasters hair to the scalp
  • Clammy skin
  • Leaning into a forceful wind
  • The sudden weight of clothing
  • Blurry vision as rain gets in one's eyes or fogs up glasses
  • A vibration in one's feet as thunder shakes the floor
  • Tense muscles as one flinches at a crack of thunder
  • A heart that races
  • Tightness in the chest
SOUNDS:
  • Thunder that booms hard enough to rattle windows
  • Rain slamming into the roof
  • Hail pinging off windows
  • A sharp crack of lightning as it strikes
  • An absence of sound as power goes out in a home
REINFORCING A MOOD:
Before a thunderstorm, the air grows heavy and feels charged. As winds pick up, many people get an unsettled, antsy feeling. In the throes of a storm, some people hide while others watch in awe, knowing they're experiencing something powerful and uncontrollable. For these reasons, thunderstorms often create a mood of oppression and unease.
SYMBOLISM:
POWER, DANGER, NATURE, REBELLION, HELPLESSNESS, OPPRESSION
COMMON CLICHÉS:
  • A clap of thunder signaling an ominous event or the arrival of a villain
  • A storm building and breaking as a plot conflict comes to a head (such as a battle)
WEATHER NOTES:
Thunderclouds require warm, moist air to form and so don't commonly occur in places where the air is mostly cool or dry. This type of storm usually happens in late spring and summer; thunderstorms differ from regular rainstorms in that they often do not last long, whereas a strong rainstorm can go on for days. Severe thunderstorms can also spawn hail and even tornadoes.
SCENARIOS FOR ADDING CONFLICT OR TENSION:
  • Thunderstorms would be a difficult scenario for a character who has high anxiety, especially if the power goes out
  • Nothing spoils an outdoor wedding, hike, or picnic like a sudden thunderstorm
  • Some tasks become extra onerous when bad weather is present. Throw an additional obstacle at your character to spoil his mood such as having to change a flat, packing the car at the end of a camping trip, or walking home when he has missed the bus
