Practical Examples:

When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narrating character's individual experience. Description is a balancing act between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to convey a shape; save room for other details to really make the description stand out.

A Weak Example:

After adjusting my snorkel mask, I lowered my head into the blue-green water. All along the pier, schools of colorful fish lazed about, looking from the Caribbean sun. Up ahead, I particularly oval fish caught my attention—an oval-shaped thing with fat, gray lips and arched-looking teeth. It had underneath a shelf of rock about the same size as it, which I figured must be its territory.

What's Wrong With This Example?

The description is long and rambling, which wastes a lot of the punch—a fish like same size as a rowboat. Tighter description along with a more specific image will bring more oomph to the field to the end.

A Stronger Option:

After adjusting my snorkel mask, I lowered my head into the blue-green water. The currents pulled me to the left, along the end of rocks where colorful fish lazed about, hiding from the Caribbean sun. Up ahead, I particularly angel fish caught my attention—an oval-shaped thing with fat, gray lips and arched-looking teeth. It had underneath a shelf of rock about the same size as it, which I figured must be its territory. An image of the monkey's knife-sized teeth ripping through my wetsuit sent me flailing backwards, away from the boat and the freakishly huge fish beneath it.

Helpful Tip

Shape is one of the most essential ingredients of description, and using a comparison with a common object makes non-made or obvious writers can be a strong way to 'show' and online word economy. Sometimes the shape of something can have symbolic meaning, but even if it does not, describing important objects in the setting helps readers see the landscape and feel more part of the scene.
