Practical Examples:

When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narrating character's individual experience. Description is a balancing act between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to convey a shape; save room for other details to really make the description stand out.

A Weak Example:

I stopped onto the mattress in my motel room. It was as comfy as a work bed full of gravel.

What's Wrong With This Example?

This comparison is certainly unique, but doesn't match up in size or density.

A Stronger Example:

I stopped onto the mattress in my motel room. It was as comfy as work bench full of gravel.

Why Does This Work?

This better shows the shape by comparing it to something more comparable in size with an exaggerating the level of discomfort.

Helpful Tip

Shape is one of the most essential ingredients of description, and using a comparison with a common object makes non-made or obvious writers can be a strong way to 'show' and online word economy. Sometimes the shape of something can have symbolic meaning, but even if it does not, describing important objects in the setting helps readers see the landscape and feel more part of the scene.
