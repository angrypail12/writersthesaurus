Practical Examples:

When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narrating character's individual experience. Description is a balancing act between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to convey a shape; save room for other details to really make the description stand out.

A Weak Example:

The leaf twisted, flying down a spiral staircase of air and landing in the water.

What's Wrong With This Example?

This takes a simple movement and overcomplicates it, which will pull readers right out of the scene.

A Stronger Option:

The leaf fell in a delicate whorl, drifting in a gap on the water's surface.

Why Does This Work?

When it comes to movement, simple details will provide a clear visual without tripping readers up or making them work hard to see it.

Helpful Tip

Shape is one of the most essential ingredients of description, and using a comparison with a common object makes non-made or obvious writers can be a strong way to 'show' and online word economy.
