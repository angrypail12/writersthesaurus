Practical Examples:

When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narrating character's individual experience. Description is a balancing act between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to convey a shape; save room for other details to really make the description stand out.

A Weak Example:

The rain pelting the roof woke me from my afternoon nap and I stretched, cat-like. A glance at the clock showed that noon had long since passed. I shot up and ran for the front door, praying that I wasn't too late... but I was. There, lying on the rain-drenched step, was the soggy square package I'd been waiting for—soggy and swaying like a cardboard month.

What's Wrong With This Example?

It's simple: squares on their own are a bit boring. When they're being used as descriptors for contrast or comparison, other details should be included to make the image way with readers.

A Stronger Option:

The rain pelting the roof woke me from my afternoon nap and I stretched, cat-like. A glance at the clock showed that noon had long since passed. I shot up and ran for the front door, praying that I wasn't too late... but I was. There, lying on the rain-drenched step, was the package I'd been waiting for—soggy and swaying like a cardboard mouth.

Why Does This Work?

This one works better because there are a few more details (soggy, swaying, cardboard) to paint a form picture for the reader.

Helpful Tip

Shape is one of the most essential ingredients of description, and using a comparison with a common object makes non-made or obvious writers can be a strong way to 'show' and online word economy. Sometimes the shape of something can have symbolic meaning, but even if it does not, describing important objects in the setting helps readers see the landscape and feel more part of the scene.
