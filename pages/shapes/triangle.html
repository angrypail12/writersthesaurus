Practical Examples:

When making comparisons, it is often beneficial to use objects that readers will recognize. It's also important to choose objects that fit both the type of story you're writing and the narrating character's individual experience. Description is a balancing act between anchoring readers in the unique story world while only describing what is necessary. Use as few words as possible to convey a shape; save room for other details to really make the description stand out.

A Weak Example:

Big Joe humbled up, blocking out my sun and holding a hot dog in each hand. If only the lunch hours could see him here at the beach, with his wedge of a Speedo hiking beneath his sprawling gut like a piece of old green trapped under a soft cushion. "Extra mustard, just like you asked," he says, waving one of the foil wrapped dogs at me.

What's Wrong With This Example?

While the shape is right, candy corn doesn't exactly fit the speedo; giant red thorns, and a boldly text comparison both Joe's image as a molester in ways that are not intended.

A Stronger Option:

Big Joe humbled up, blocking out my sun and holding a hot dog in each hand. If only the lunch hours could see him here at the beach, with his wedge of a Speedo hiking beneath his sprawling gut like a slice of old green trapped under a soft cushion. "Extra mustard, just like you asked," he says, waving one of the foil wrapped dogs at me.

Why Does This Work?

Old pizza and a couch are much better comparisons, both in content and size.

Helpful Tip

Shape is one of the most essential ingredients of description, and using a comparison with a common object makes non-made or obvious writers can be a strong way to 'show' and online word economy. Sometimes the shape of something can have symbolic meaning, but even if it does not, describing important objects in the setting helps readers see the landscape and feel more part of the scene.
